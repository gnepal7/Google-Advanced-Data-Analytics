# Critical data security and privacy principles
 - Data ethics governs responsible collection, sharing, and use of data.
 - Personally identifiable information must be carefully protected.
 - Data privacy and security must be maintained throughout data lifecycle.
+ Privacy matters
 - Protection from unauthorized access to their private data
 - Freedom from inappropriate use of their data
 - The right to inspect, update, or correct their data
 - Ability to give consent to data collection
 - Legal right to access the data

+ Protecting privacy with data anonymization 
 - Access permissions restrict data to authorized users only.
 - Data anonymization removes personally identifiable information (PII).
 - Techniques include masking, hashing, and data replacement.
 - Data aggregation summarizes information from many users.
 - These methods improve privacy and ensure compliance standards.
 - Sensitive data related to PII include:
  . Telephone numbers
  . Names
  . License plates and license numbers
  . Social security numbers
  . IP addresses
  . Medical records
  . Email addresses
  . Photographs
  . Account numbers

# The practices and principles of good data stewardship
 + Respect privacy
  - PII is information that can identify individuals directly or indirectly.
  - Organizations have a responsibility to protect user privacy.
  - Users trust companies with their personal data.
  - Data professionals must handle personal data with care.
  - Data privacy laws guide best practices globally.
  - Professionals must stay updated on changing regulations.
  - General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR): EU law protecting data privacy and security.
  - Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados Pessoais (LGPD): Brazilian law regulating personal data use.
  - California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA): Gives California consumers control over personal data.
  - Other U.S. states also enforce similar privacy protection laws.

 + Be cautious of unintentional harm
  - Data analytics influences decisions across many industries.
  - Data-driven decisions can impact social and economic factors.
  - Accuracy and respect for cultural norms are important in analysis.
  - Global markets require awareness of differing cultural impacts.
  - Data professionals should follow company policies and seek leadership guidance.

+ Avoid creating or reinforcing bias
 - Data bias can be difficult to identify in analysis.
 - Data collection is influenced by human perspectives and experiences.
 - Different backgrounds and beliefs can introduce bias into data.
 - Bias can affect results and business decision-making.

+ Consider inclusivity
 - Data collection methods may exclude certain populations.
 - Inclusive approaches improve data collection and analysis quality.
 - Diverse teams and equity-focused analysis benefit all stakeholders.

+ Uphold high standards of scientific excellence
 - Data analytics is grounded in the scientific method.
 - Inquiry, collaboration, and feedback improve analytical outcomes.
 - AI depends on instructions provided by data professionals.
 - More careful analysis leads to better results.
 - Different industries follow different data standards and conventions.
 - Industry priorities vary, such as transportation vs healthcare use cases.
 - Examples include route prediction in transport and medical analysis in healthcare.

+ Data stewardship and ethics conversations
 - Data professionals must address ethical issues like bias and PII protection.
 - Protecting user data and privacy is a key responsibility.
 - Professionals often seek guidance from online data communities.
 - Shared experiences help solve similar ethical challenges.
