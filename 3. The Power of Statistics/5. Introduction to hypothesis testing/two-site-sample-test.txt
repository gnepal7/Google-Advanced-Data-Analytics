# Two-sample tests: Means
  + Two-sample t-test for means assumptions
    - The two samples are independent of each other
    - For each sample, the data is drawn randomly from a normally distributed population
    - The population standard deviation is unknown
  + Hypothesis
    - Null: There is no difference in the mean time spent on version A and version B
    - Alternative: There is a difference in the mean time spent on version A and version B
  + Significance level = 5%
  + P-value
    - The probability of observing a difference in your sample means as or more extreme than the
      difference observed whtn the null hypothesis is true
  + Draw a conclusion
    - if p-value < significance level: reject the null hypothesis
    - if p-value > significance level: fail to reject the null hypothesis
    . Conclusion : Fail to rejcet the null hypothesis

# One-tailed and two-tailed tests
  + One-tailed test
    - One-tailed test: Alternative hypothesis specifies parameter is greater or less.
    - Left-tailed test: Alternative hypothesis states parameter is less than null value.
    - Right-tailed test: Alternative hypothesis states parameter is greater than null value.
  + Two-tailed test
    - Two-tailed test: Alternative hypothesis states parameter is not equal to null value.
    - One-tailed tests: Test whether parameter is specifically greater or less.
    - Symbols: Left-tailed (<), right-tailed (>), two-tailed (≠).
  + One-tailed versus two-tailed
    - One-tailed tests provide greater power to detect effects in one direction.
    - They do not test for effects in the opposite direction.
    - Consider consequences of missing effects in the untested direction.
    - One-tailed tests may miss harmful or unfavorable outcomes.
    - Appropriate when missing opposite-direction effects has minimal consequences.
    - Useful when only one specific direction is important to test.

# A/B testing 
  + A typical A/B test has at least three main features:
    1. Test design
    2. Sampling
    3. Hypothesis testing
  + Randomized controlled experiment 
    - A/B test: Basic form of a randomized controlled experiment.
    - Subjects are randomly assigned to control and treatment groups.
    - Control group: Not exposed to the treatment.
    - Treatment group: Exposed to the new change being tested.
    - Difference between groups measures the treatment's effect.
    - Keeping treatment as the only difference enables causal conclusions.

# Experimental Design
  + Define your variables
    - Independent variable: 
      > Controlled or changed factor used to investigate its effect on outcomes.
    - Dependent variable: 
      > Measured outcome whose value is influenced by the independent variable.
  + Formulate your hypothesis
    - Hypothesis: 
      > Predicts relationship between independent and dependent variables.
    - Null hypothesis (H₀): 
      > Assumes no effect or relationship in the population.
    - Alternative hypothesis (H₁): 
      > Assumes an effect or relationship exists.
    + Assign test subjects to treatment and control groups
      - Controlled experiment: 
        > Compares treatment and control groups to measure an effect.
      - Treatment group: 
        > Exposed to the new change being tested.
      - Control group: 
        > Not exposed to the treatment.
      - Difference in group metrics estimates the treatment’s effect.
      . Randomization 
        - Assigns subjects randomly to reduce effects of other factors.
      . Common methods: 
        - Completely randomized design and randomized block design.
